Understanding Raccoon Behavior and Common Problems

Raccoons are fascinating creatures, known for their intelligence and adaptability. However, their opportunistic nature and ability to thrive in urban and suburban environments can lead to significant conflicts with humans. Understanding their behavior is key to effective management and prevention.

Nocturnal Habits

Raccoons are primarily nocturnal, meaning they are most active during the night. This is why you often hear them moving around in your attic or walls after dark. Their nighttime foraging can lead to overturned garbage cans, damaged gardens, and disturbance of outdoor pets. If you hear scratching, thumping, or chattering sounds in your home during the night, it's a strong indicator of raccoon activity.

Searching for Shelter

Especially during colder months or when seeking a safe place to raise their young, raccoons will actively look for warm, secure dens. Attics, chimneys, crawl spaces, and even sheds or garages provide ideal shelter. Once inside, they can cause extensive damage by nesting, chewing on electrical wires, and soiling insulation with urine and feces. This damage can be costly to repair and can also create fire hazards.

Foraging for Food

Raccoons are omnivores with a varied diet. They are attracted to easily accessible food sources, including:

  • Garbage
  • Pet food left outdoors
  • Ripe fruits and vegetables in gardens
  • Bird seed
  • Compost piles

Securing these food sources is a crucial step in preventing raccoons from being attracted to your property in the first place. Simple measures like using sturdy, self-latching garbage cans can make a big difference in deterring them.

Communicating Through Vocalizations

Raccoons communicate through a variety of sounds, including purring, chattering, growling, and shrieking. Hearing these vocalizations, particularly in or around your home, is a clear sign of their presence. Mother raccoons and their kits are particularly vocal.

Disease Transmission Risks

Raccoons can carry and transmit a number of diseases and parasites that are harmful to humans and pets, including rabies and raccoon roundworm (Baylisascaris). Coming into contact with their feces or saliva can pose a health risk. Proper handling and cleanup of areas contaminated with raccoon waste is essential and should be done with caution, ideally by professionals. This is another critical reason why professional removal and sanitation is recommedned.

Signs You Need Raccoon Removal

Being aware of the signs of a raccoon infestation is the first step in addressing the problem quickly and efficiently. If you notice any of the following indicators, contacting a professional for raccoon removal in Woodbury, MN is highly recommended.

Unusual Noises

Hearing scratching, thumping, chattering, or vocalizations in your attic, walls, chimney, or crawl spaces, particularly at night, is a strong indicator that you have unwanted wildlife visitors, quite possibly raccoons. These sounds become more pronounced when kits are present.

Damaged Structures

Raccoons are strong and persistent. Look for signs of damage to your home's exterior, such as:

  • Torn or ripped soffits, fascia, or siding
  • Loose or missing shingles on the roof
  • Bent or damaged vents
  • Gnaw marks on wood or other materials
  • Damage to chimneys (loose bricks or crumbling mortar)

This type of damage not only allows raccoons access but can also lead to water damage and other structural issues if not repaired promptly.

Overturned Garbage Cans

This is a classic sign of raccoon activity. If your securely closed garbage cans are consistently being raided and overturned, raccoons are very likely the culprits. They are attracted to the smell of food waste and are strong enough to manipulate even heavy containers. Seeing scattering garbage around your property is a tell-tale sign.

Presence of Droppings

Raccoon droppings are typically dark, tubular, and can contain remnants of their diet. They often establish communal defecation sites, known as latrines, which can be found on decks, patios, in attics, or near the base of trees. Raccoon feces can contain harmful pathogens, so it's essential to handle them with extreme caution and ideally leave the cleanup to professionals. The presence of latrines is a strong indicator of a persistent problem.

Damaged Gardens or Lawns

Raccoons may dig up lawns or gardens in search of grubs, insects, or other food sources. They might also damage bird feeders or outdoor water features. If your landscaping looks disturbed or damaged overnight, raccoons could be responsible. This foraging behavior can cause significant aesthetic and monetary damage to your property.

Seeing Raccoons During the Day

While raccoons are primarily nocturnal, seeing one during the day isn't always a cause for alarm. It could be a mother foraging for food to feed her young. However, if a raccoon appears disoriented, aggressive, or shows other unusual behavior during daylight hours, it could be a sign of illness, such as rabies, and should be avoided at all costs. It's always safer to contact professionals if you encounter a raccoon behaving strangely.

Beyond Removal: Preventing Future Wildlife Issues

Our commitment to your safety and peace of mind extends beyond simply removing the immediate raccoon problem. We believe in a proactive approach to wildlife management, helping you implement strategies to prevent future intrusions from raccoons and other common wildlife found in Woodbury, like squirrels or even larger animals requiring wildlife control. Our exclusion services are a key component of this prevention strategy.

Securing Your Home's Exterior

Think of your home's exterior as its first line of defense against wildlife. We can help you identify and seal potential entry points that you might not even be aware of. This includes repairing or reinforcing areas such as:

  • Roof eaves and gables
  • Chimney caps and screens
  • Vents of all types (bath, kitchen, dryer, attic, foundation)
  • Gaps around utility pipes and wires entering the home
  • Loose siding or trim
  • Unsealed foundation cracks

By making your home's exterior impermeable to wildlife, you significantly reduce the chances of a future infestation. This preventative maintenance not only keeps wildlife out but also protects your home from weather-related damage.

Managing Attractants on Your Property

Remember, raccoons are drawn to accessible food sources. By making simple changes to your outdoor habits, you can make your property less appealing to them. This includes:

  • Securing garbage cans with tight-fitting lids or investing in animal-proof containers.
  • Bringing pet food indoors, especially overnight.
  • Harvesting ripe fruits and vegetables from your garden promptly.
  • Using bird feeders that are difficult for raccoons to access or bringing feeders in at night.
  • Maintaining compost piles properly, avoiding meat or dairy scraps that attract wildlife.
  • Cleaning up spilled food outdoors immediately.

These simple steps, when combined with our professional exclusion services, provide a robust defense against raccoon intrusions. Reducing available food sources significantly decreases the likelihood of wildlife investigating your property as a potential habitat.

Addressing Landscaping Issues

overgrown bushes or trees that provide cover near your home, or tree branches that hang over your roof can offer raccoons easy access to your attic or upper levels. Keeping trees and shrubs trimmed away from your home's structure can act as a deterrent and eliminate easy climbing routes. Consider removing fallen fruit from trees promptly to avoid attracting foragers. Additionally, ensuring that your deck or shed doesn't have accessible undersides can prevent raccoons from denning there.

Educating Yourself and Your Family

Understanding the typical behaviors of raccoons and other local wildlife empowers you to be more aware of potential risks and take preventative measures. Teach children about the dangers of approaching wild animals and the importance of not leaving food sources readily available outdoors. Knowledge is a powerful tool in preventing human-wildlife conflicts. Being observant of your surroundings and promptly addressing potential issues can save you considerable trouble down the line.

This proactive step can prevent costly damage and stressful encounters in the future, ultimately saving you time, money, and worry.
